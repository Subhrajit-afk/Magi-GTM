The 46 questions nobody has a workflow for

Sales and marketing don't fail at strategy. They fail at forty-six small questions asked every morning that nothing in the stack answers. This maps all of them — sorted by what it costs when the answer is wrong, not by how often it's asked. Thirty of the forty-six are mechanical. That's the opportunity. The other sixteen are judgment or incentives, and automating them makes things worse.

Severity tier

Ranked by blast radius — does a wrong answer poison other answers downstream? Not by frequency. "Asked often" is not the same as "expensive when wrong."

Tier 1 · Critical. Dependency roots and silent failures. Everything below is unreliable until these are answered.
Tier 2 · High. Directly costs pipeline. Several have published failure rates behind them.
Tier 3 · Medium. Real friction, contained blast radius.
Tier 4 · Hands-off. Not low-stakes — low-automatable. Judgment calls. Building these produces confident wrong answers.

What's shaky here

Read before acting on it

Four things to hold loosely

  1. The question list is derived, not observed. Nothing published measures what these roles ask daily. Thirty-three of forty-six are marked Inferred. Validate with real users before building — filter to Inferred to see the exact set.
  2. The evidence base is vendor-dominated. Nearly every source on sales–marketing gaps is published by a company selling the fix. Where numbers appear in the row detail, the strongest are Harvard Business Review (2011), MIT/InsideSales (2007), Forrester and Gartner — and the Forrester/Gartner reports are paywalled and only seen secondhand. Several widely-quoted stats in this space have no traceable primary source at all.
  3. Frequency is guessed. Everything is labelled a daily question. Some are plainly weekly, some event-driven. For workflow design, cadence matters as much as the question text — and this map doesn't have it.
  4. Company size is unspecified and it changes the shape. Below roughly twenty people, the manager and ops questions collapse or vanish, and "what's the context on this account" is answered by turning around in a chair. This map assumes a team large enough for handoffs to be real. If that's wrong, the tiering changes materially.
